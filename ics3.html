Topic: Anonymity

Introduction

As we venture into the age of the internet and technology, many issues that we face today deal with the term “anonymity” and how it relates to the broader internet. An anon can make a comment, and voice opinions without the slightest tie to their own personal identity. They have extreme freedom while being completely unacknowledged and unknown. We are at a crossroads where we have to decide if the internet is even safe for its intended users. How and why did this become such a problem and why should we be more concerned about the repercussions society face as we try and navigate the cyber net?

Internet Anonymity

First, let's analyze the psychology behind human grouping and how it ties into the internet and anonymity. In 1969, a Standford professor conducted a social experiment where he gave female students something to mask their identity and some with nametags that revealed their identities. They were told to give an electric shock to participants and were allowed to refuse if they wanted to. The study revealed that the female students were twice as likely to shock the participants if their identity was hidden. What does this show? Zimbardo's study showed that there is a link between how people treat others when they are under the protection of anonymity. In fact, many other studies concluded that those with their personal info concealed are more likely aggressively/illegally. So in a sense, the internet shows that people tend to show their true colors on the internet compared to real-life interactions, as there are no real consequences for being who you are on the internet. So how does this directly correlate with the internet? In many ways actually, as most interactions online are with completely anonymous people. In video games especially there is an especially flux of toxic/bullies that seem to rule their given game. Gamers are more likely to rage at their teammates and curse them out as opposed to a sport like a basketball. We rarely see teammates blow up at each other simply because there is a barrier that doesn't allow that kind of behavior. Games such as Overwatch and League of Legends are prolific within the gaming community for their toxic communities. Team-based games are especially bad since they require communication and coordination in order to play the game, so simply turning off the chat affects the gameplay and enjoyment. League of Legends had gotten so bad in terms of bullying and harassment that they opted to remove the /all chat feature in matchmaking. "While /all chat can be the source of fun social interaction between teams, as well as some good-hearted banter, right now negative interactions outweigh the positives," said the lead director Andrei van Roon and lead gameplay producer Jeremy Lee. "We know this sucks for those of you who just want to compliment your lane opponent's skin or ask for a dance party in Baron pit. But we believe the trade-off is worth it to cut down on the growing negativity /all chat has been creating in your games." In Riot Korea users even have to sign up with their Social Security as a way to reduce anonymity in their game. The toxicity kills the passion to play a game, and one user reports that “I make one mistake and I’m getting flamed (made fun of/gaslighted). I can’t enjoy the game I once loved due to this community”. This sentiment is shared, with whole gaming groups demanding harsher bans for those that are reported to be toxic. Overwatch, which was once the “Game of the Year”, now sits as a barren game that was killed due to its lack of community control and action towards toxic players. In more recent times, many girls that choose to game experienced cyberbullying because of the simple fact that they are girls. According to a Young Gamers and Gamblers Education Trust study, nearly 40 percent of female gamers had received inappropriate and explicit messages from users. The same amount also admitted to being verbally harassed through voice communication and 30 percent have been sexually harassed by other games. Due to the cloak that the internet gives to misogynists, they are able to do these things with no risk.

Crowd Mentality

So how does crowd mentality play into the broader picture? Many studies have concluded that being in a crowd, in a way, is almost like having an identity concealer. In 1981, Leo Mann showed that just being in a crowd not only added to offensive behavior but also violent behavior. His case studied numerous suicide attempts and found out that in most instances they included a group of people, and the individual was encouraged to harm himself by the crowd. People encouraged the suicidal person to jump, and in three of the instances actually jeered when rescuers prevented the death. Even in one instance, the crowd threw obscenities and jeered at the rescuers. Some of the factors that allow crowds like this to form are the physical distance from the actual event(enabling their jeers to be heard but leaving faces impossible to identify) and the time of day that the event was taking palace. If it was night times, onlookers can rest assured as it's almost like a mask due to the low visibility. Group behavior like this is all too alive on the internet and even though people aren’t physically together, crowd mentality is still replicated and in some cases is even worse. Subgroups like 4Chan are considered a cesspool for homophobia, racism, sexism, etc. The people in this group are mostly anonymous users that seem to compete for who can do the most obscene and disgusting things to new users. Their horrors include trolling and harassing the parents of a team that recently killed themselves and using epilepsy discussion boards to flood them with flashing images in order to induce a seizure in someone. The final straw for the mods of their group was when site operators had stepped in to block child pornography from being distributed on the site. Bully behavior is something that we can’t control on the internet. They are no teachers, policing or parents to go to. These behaviors especially inflict tolls on our youth. Research shows that there are many other significant consequences of cyberbullying to our young, such as high rates of mental health issues like depression and anxiety, low self-esteem, difficulties sleeping, physical issues such as headaches, and an increased amount of eating disorders especially prevalent among young girls. These are not problems a young child should face on the internet, and the root of the cause is the internet itself. It allows for some truly disgusting people to run rampage. In 2008 a teenager killed herself after her boyfriend sent her nude images to other teenagers in several Ohio high schools. She was relentlessly tormented and harassed on popular social media websites such as MySpace. In a nearly identical fashion, a 13-year-old killed herself a year later due to similar reasons. The ease of access to the internet and the lack of identity allow for these cruel acts to occur

Dealing with the Issue

So how do we help alleviate this situation? My personal thought on this is to refrain from using the internet as a social gateway, at least till a certain age. No matter what you do, there is no getting rid of internet anonymity as they are many advanced ways to bypass any IP/Location detection. Companies do their best, and with all this toxicity and bullying, companies are trying to find ways to combat this issue. Instagram introduced the private mode which allowed users to completely hide their accounts from outside users and required requests for DM’s. They also started enforcing an age policy only allowing those 13 and up to create an account. Riot Games started filtering out words such as “kys” in order to keep the chat in line and has never allowed voice chat in their games. But even through all of this, it still remains the problem. The best way to protect against this problem is for us peers to identify those who are affected and help them out. Psychologists say that the early signs of cyberbullying are angry moods after being online, reluctance to be with family or friends, refusing to go to school, and showing signs of depression or sadness. We also need to promote better behavior in our youths, and I think the best way is the “golden rule”. Treat others how you would like to be treated. The disgusting acts I have read about, are not human nature. This is a consequence of the internet and how it provides a mask for those to act however they want. True behaviors are shown and it is up to us to help solve this issue. The internet is still young, and as we progress we can make behaviors like this ancient history. I'll leave off on one quote, “The solution to problematic behavior of crowds and groups is to challenge and change toxic group norms.” Fixing the problem of online aggression, then, maybe a matter of figuring out how to apply a social mold that has in everyday life.”.
